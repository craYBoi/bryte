Bryte Photographer Guide

This guide is created for Bryte student photographers to use. The images and the text content in this guide are for internal training purpose only. Please do not share with other people.

Hi there, We welcome you to the Bryte Photo family!

This guide has everything to get you started in addition to in person training. It also can be served as a manual where you can find most the answers to your questions. Looks like there's a lot.. But don't worry! We've simplied the entire portrait photography and broken down the whole thing into 8 short sections, that are really easy to digest. Its topic ranges from the very basic theories of a great headshot photos, to how do you actually run a complate photoshoot.

So, let's get started!

>> 1. Equipments & Usage

What's in the Bryte Kit?

  • 3 85-Watts florescent light bulbs (1 light bulb for backup) + 2 lighting stands

    Place the lightbulb on the stand using the little swivel, and it becomes a "studio light". One lighting is to used as a Key Light along with the reflective umbrella. Key light is the most important light in portrait photography, since it is the strongest. The other light is served as the Background Light, which is placed right behind the client to light up the chromakey

    Lighting is undoubtedly the most important component of a studio portrait photography, because it determines how the lighting behaves on person's face. A good position of key light can create beautiful contrast and rich layers on person's face.

  • 1 black/silver umbrella

    The light with the reflective black/silver umbrella attached is our key light. The umbrella is able to reflect 100% of the light emitted from the key light bulb into opposite direction, and create a unique lighting with strong specular effect.

    Some of the reflected light goes to the client, and light up the majority of the person. Some hit the Reflector placed right beside the person on the opposite side of the key light, and then is reflected onto the dark side of the person.

  • 1 reflector + 1 reflector stand + 1 reflector clip

    The shiny circular thing is our Reflector. Its job is to reflect part of the light emitted from key light, and shines up the dark side of the person’s face. It could be considered as the Fill Light alternative. A good position of the reflector can create a natural transition of the intensity and contrast on the person's face instead of a harsh and dramatic one.

  • Green backdrop (Chromakey) + backdrop stands + 6 backdrop clips

    Chromakey is a standard green background canvas, usually made of muslin. The green background of the photo taken at the photoshoot will be translated into various different background options during post processing.

  • 1 Whiteboard + 2 whiteboard markers + 1 wiping cloth

    The whiteboard is for you to write down client's email before starting the headshot session. This will make sure you can keep track of photos when you are uploading them onto Dropbox.

  • 1 25-ft power extension cord + 1 Bryte Photo Tee + 1 mirror + Posters

    Sometimes if there are no power outlets near you, the 25-ft extension cord is always here to the rescue! We also prepared you with our Bryte gear! Along with couple posters that will need to be hang up and a mirror for our clients to brush up in the waiting area during the photoshoot.

>> 2. The 4 most important things about a great headshot photo

"A great headshot photo always looks natural"

First, Lighting!

The lighting on the person’s face is so important that you can't imagine a good headshot photo without good and natural lighting on it. The lighting on the face comes from 3 sources: Key light, Reflector (Fill Light) and the environment.

Out of all the light sources, Key light and Reflector (Fill Light) are the most essential components and the most fundamental basis of a great headshot photo.

In a great headshot photo:

  1. The person’s face is always well-lit
  2. Make sure not to cast long shadows on the face

Some bad examples of the lighting:

The face is not well lit (The reflector needs to be adjusted since the dark side of the face remains dark, and the key light also needs to be adjusted since it fails to light up the majority of the face.)

There are large areas of shadows around the eyes. (The angle and intensity of the light from key light needs to be modified)

Second, Color!

Color means the color balance of the photo. It’s determined by both the White Balance settings (WB) on your DSLR, and the environment. The tricky point is that: since the environment is not going to be constant everytime, sometimes it tend to be warm and sometimes cold. So being able to adjust the White Balance to complement the environment color is the key.

Both Nikon and Canon has pre-set white balance settings. It is suggested to use Auto White Balance (AWB) at first. If the photo still looks too red/yellow/blue…, you can use one of the pre-set White Balance setting (e.g "Tungsten", "Cloudy"..). Here's a simple diagram showing the difference between some of these white balance presets.

As you can see, These White Balance presets create a quite different color balance on the photo. "Tungsten" gives a very cold touch, since it's assuming the environment is of tungsten light, which is very warm, so it add coldness to the photo to compensate the extra warmness tungsten environment creates. Usually settings auto white balance will do the work, but it's always good to know how to manually adjust the photo to the right color balance when your DSLR can't do a great job detecting it.

One trick to spot whether the color balance is good is to look at the color of the green backdrop, if it's not perfectly green, it means the entire photo has some other colors involved.

Third, Composition!

Composition stands for how you place the client on the photo canvas. To achieve a perfect composition, consider following points:

  1. Always make sure the head is horizontally centered on the canvas
  2. Leave a little space above the head
  3. Down to cover the entire collar/tie knot/short necklaces

Some bad examples of compositions:

too zoomed in, didn't cover the entire collar

too zoomed out, showing too much of body

Leaving too much space above

The head is not horizontally centered

Last but not least, Alignment!

Showing a lot of chin is never a good idea. Always place your DSLR on the same height or slightly above the client's eye level. Never shoot from below!

>> 3. DSLR Settings

  • Camera Mode: Manual

    Let’s do manual, because it sounds cool.. haha not exactly, because in some situations leaving decisions to DSLRs is not always a good idea, and almost everything is static during headshot photography, so usually you don't need to adjust the settings once they are good.

  • Shutter Speed: 1/200

    It’s recommended that you always keep the shuttle speed around 1/200, which will always allow you to take crisp, clear photos, even if there's a slight movement.

  • Aperture / f-number: 5.6

    Let’s set the f number to 5.6 instead of typically lower numbers(2.2, 2.5, 2.8), which is an available option for almost all DSLRs/lens, and it also has a good depth of field effect and also allows more room for you to focus on the object, at the same time still allowing a decent amount of light hitting the censor.

  • White Balance: Depends (Usually set to Auto: AWB)

    To compensate the environment color tone, you will need to adjust the White Balance after you finish setting up the lighting. Please check the examples of good color in The 4 most important things about a great headshot photo for reference.

  • Focus: AF (Auto Focus), One Shot Mode

    Auto Focus is always preferred for portrait photography in studio settings. Make sure to set the AF mode to One Shot, since basically everything is still, so we only want to focus once with great accuracy. AI SERVO mode works better for moving object, since it can track and predict the movement, and constantly adjust the focus. The tradeoff is that it's not as accurate as One Shot.

  • ISO: Depends

    To make sure the photo doesn't look too dark/bright, you will need to adjust the ISO after you finish setting up the lightings and equipment. It's not recommended to set ISO to auto because in most cases that value turns out to be a little lower than it should. Make sure to adjust ISO according to the brightness standard. >> 4. Equipment Setup

First step towards taking a great headshot photo is to place every piece of equipment in the right place. Here we have a very comprehensive instruction on how and where to place different parts of equipment. The layout of these equipment is served to give a perfect lighting with smooth and natural contrast on the client's face.

  • Starting with the green backdrop

    1. Get the stands for green backdrop ready and hang up the green backdrop.
    2. Use 6 backdrop clips to solidify, and make sure most of the wrinkles on the backdrops are gone.

  • Background light

    1. Place the background light facing the backdrop
    2. keep it 0.5 meters away from the backdrop
    3. Keep it pointing a little bit downward, in order to make sure the light doesn't show up in the photos you take.

  • The Stool/Chair

    1. Place the stool/chair 0.5 meters away from the background light (1 meter away from the backdrop)

  • The Key light (Important)

    1. Place the keylight according to the diagram below.
    2. Make sure the tip of the reflective umbrella is pointing directly to the background light. Not the person.
    3. Make sure to keep the angle of the umbrella 30 degrees downward.
    4. Keep the key light 1 meter away from the person
    5. It's not recommend to adjust the key light after the initial setup.

    Vertical View

    Side View

    Front View (From photographer's perspective)

  • Reflector

    1. The reflector is usually placed right besides the person and is relatively closer to the person than the key light.
    2. Make sure the reflector has an upward angle, so that it can reflect the light emitted from key light.
    3. You will need to adjust the reflector basically everytime a new client goes in, or even between different poses, because it is very sensitive to change of location. A small change of the person's location will make a huge difference.
    4. Remember to always keep an eye on the client's face when you are adjusting the reflector. Since a small change of the position of the reflector can make a huge different, it's important to make sure to keep the reflector's position and angle when you see the other side of the client's face are well lit.

    Without reflector

    With reflector

  • Tripod & DSLR

    1. Place the tripod & DSLR to achieve the perfect composition mentioned in “The 4 most important things about a great headshot photo”
    2. Make sure the camera is at the same height of or slightly above the person’s eye level

  • Waiting Area

    1. If the photoshoots take place in career centers, there are usually abundant space for you to set up the waiting area.
    2. Make sure to find a table, where you can put the mirror and the computer/tablet for students to check in
    3. Find some chairs around the table. Make sure to make it feel like a waiting area.
    4. Make sure to hang up the posters on the wall around the mirror.

>> 5. The Expressions and Poses

Guess what, the ideal camera settings and photo composition are not the whole story of an amazing headshot. The core of the photo is actually the actual person in the headshot photo! It’s the energy one could feel from that person: the Pose, the Expression and some extra spices.

To make everything simple, there are 2 poses, "Straight on" and "Side". Straight on means that every part of the person is facing towards the camera. Side can have 2 options, "Turn to the right" or "Turn to the left". In a side pose, the person usually has his or her body turned to one side approximately 45 degrees, and turn their face back to the camera.

If you think about it, one's expression can be simply viewed as a combination of the person's eye and the mouth. A person can look serious, with lips closed, no smile (mouth movement) and intensely focused eyes (eye movement), and these makes a "Serious Expression". There are a few expressions we listed below.

In addition, it turns out that tilting one's head from one side to the other a little bit is pretty popular among the girls. Sometimes, a good head tilt can emit extra confidence and charisma.

Pose

  1. Straight on
  2. Side (Either Left or Right)

Expression

  1. No Expression (Mouth: completely relaxed. Eyes: keep it natural)
  2. Serious Expression (Mouth: No smile, lip closed. Eyes: intensely focused)
  3. Confident Expression (Mouth: Subtle smile, lip closed. Eyes: focused)
  4. Natural Expression (Mouth: Normal natural smile, lips open. Eyes: natural and relaxed)
  5. Happy Expression (Mouth: Big Smile, lips open. Eyes: Happy eyes)

Extra

  1. Tilt the head a little bit from one side to the other, coyly towards the camera
  2. Lower the chin
  3. Make sure the person makes a Natural Expression

>> 6. Interact with Clients

Good interactions with clients will always lead to a better photography experience and headshot quality. By interacting with your customer, you will make them feel relaxed and confident, and at the same time learn more about that person, for example what type best suits that person and direct him/ her according to your judgement and intuition. If you see that person feel very comfortable doing the Confident Expression and looks really good, then you want to let that person know and do more. Keep in mind that most of the people don’t even know how to smile in front of a camera, and just as a professional photographer, you are like their mentor to guide them through this adventure!

First rule of thumb

Be confident yourself! People can feel that they are put into good hands and will be more relaxed and confident.

Second

Greet and chat with customers as soon as you call them up. This will eliminate the awkwardness and make people feel relaxed and trusting.

Third

Always give compliments. No one can reject good compliments. Make sure to give out some compliments in between the poses and expressions during a headshot session. That will make people feel more confident in front of the camera.

Within a photo session

People love varieties of poses and expressions! So let’s make sure they can have them all during the photoshoot! In order to be natural and easy for clients to follow, we came up with a simplified list of poses/expressions and corresponding cues you could say to the clients that work very well and in addition, some extra head tilt to spice things up.

  • Before posing the client

    1. Make sure to clean up the client's shirt, sweater, suit, tie or collar
    2. Make sure things like necklaces are centered on the client
    3. Make sure the client doesn't show significant amount of stray hair

  • Do Straight On pose first

    1. Start with No Expression (Mouth: completely relaxed. Eyes: keep it natural) to make the client relaxed

      Cues: sit straight and look at my camera. Try to relax your face and don't show any expressions

    2. Do Serious Expression (Mouth: No smile, lip closed. Eyes: intensely focused)

      Cues: Try to keep this expression and at the same time give me a really strong gaze.

    3. Do Confident Expression (Mouth: Subtle smile, lip closed. Eyes: focused)

      Cues: Keep the expression, and give me a subtle smile with you lips closed. Just a little smile.

    4. Do Natural Expression (Mouth: Normal natural smile, lips open. Eyes: natural and relaxed)

      Cues: Now try to open you lips and smile a little bit

    5. Also Do a head tilt while on Natural Expression for girls

      Cues: Keep the smile and everything, and try to tilt your head a little from one side to the other, and lower your chin a little bit

    6. Do Happy Expression (Mouth: Big Smile, lips open. Eyes: Happy eyes)

      Cues: Now try to give me a happy smile. Think about the moment when you walked out of the last final exam

  • Then do Side pose

    1. Repeat the same steps listed when doing "Straight on".

Example of complete steps within a photo session

  1. Call the next client up in the waiting area according to the check-in list
  2. Write the email of the next client on the whiteboard, and mark the entry on the signup list
  3. Introduce yourself, speak with CONFIDENCE. “Hey I’m [name]. I’m your photographer, and you look awesome today!”
  4. Hand the white board to the client. You could say something like “Please have a seat, and hold this whiteboard for me, I will just need to take a quick snap of it”
  5. Tell the client to put the whiteboard aside
  6. Give a quick check on the client’s clothes, ties, hairs... If they are not good, you can ask them “would you mind if I clean up your tie a little bit?”
  7. Make sure to adjust the tripod to keep the camera and the eyes on the same level, or you can shoot from above, but DEFINITELY NOT from below.
  8. Remind the client to sit straight and looks towards your camera. You could say say “Let’s do straight on pose first, focus on my camera and sit straight.”
  9. 1st shot: “Try to relax your face, and try not to give me any expressions at all.” (NO EXPRESSION)
  10. 2nd shot: “Very good! Let’s try to be a little more serious, keep this expression and try to give me a intense stare.” (SERIOUS EXPRESSION)
  11. 3rd shot: “Perfect! Now try to smile just a little bit, with you lips closed” (CONFIDENT EXPRESSION)
  12. 4th shot: “Awesome, Now keep the smile and try to open you lips and show a little bit teeth” (Some clients may not feel comfortable showing their teeth. If they insist, you could just take some more with their lips closed.) (Natural Expression)
  13. 5th shot: “Now try to tilt your head to your left, and lower your chin a little bit” (TILT HEAD ON NATURAL EXPRESSION)
  14. 6th shot: “Could you puff out your chest, and give me a gaze. That will make you look more confident. Now open your lips and try to give me a bigger smile!” (HAPPY EXPRESSION)
  15. 7th shot: “You look really good. Now think about the best vacation you ever had” (any fancy cues that will make people smile, others like “Pretend you just walked out the last final of the semester” etc...) (HAPPY EXPRESSION)
  16. 8th shot: “Great, keep the smile and let’s take 1 more!”
  17. Ask the client if he or she has a better side of the face
  18. Make the client turn to the better side, “Could you turn to your left/right like 45 degrees and looks back at my camera?”
  19. repeat steps 9 ~ 16
  20. Once the session is finished, you could move towards the client and say “Hey I think we got some good photos there, and we will send you an email to all the photo we took very soon”!) If they ask for more details, here’s the answer to that “You will be able to select 1 headshot photo for free without touchup, and there are also extra photos and touchup service available for additional purchase”

>> 7. Running the Photoshoot

Once you communicate with your photographer trainer, and agreed to do a photoshoot with Bryte, here's a complete list of instructions on the process of running the photoshoot.

3 Days before your photoshoot

  • Check your headshot kit to make sure it is fully stocked. It should have:

    • 2 lighting stands + 3 85 Watts florescent light bulbs (1 light bulb for backup)
    • 1 black/silver umbrella
    • 1 reflector + 1 reflector stand + 1 reflector clipe
    • 1 green backdrop + 1 backdrop stand + 6 backdrop clips
    • 1 25-ft extension cord
    • 1 Bryte Tee
    • 1 big bag to hold everything
    • 1 tripod
    • Check that your DSLR is fully charged
    • There should be at least 10 GB space available on the SD card
    • The lens and DSLR you use for taking headshot are fully functional

  • Slack your trainer to confirm

    Make sure to slack your trainer to confirm that your equipment is ready and steady for the photoshoot. If anything is in need for replacement, we will be able to replace it before the photoshoot.

1 Day before your photoshoot

We’ll slack the link to the check-in sheet the night before the photoshoot. This online check-in list is in Google Sheet, and you will be able to view the check-in list during the photoshoot to know who to call up next, and mark the people you already take headshot with. For the marking to work on your mobile, you will need to install Google Sheet app

Slack us to confirm that you have read this information and are ready to take headshots within 60 minutes of us Slacking you. If you do not Slack us within 60 minutes then we will assume you are not ready to do the photoshoot. This also means that you have violated the contract you signed.

45 Minutes before your shoot

By now you should have arrived at the photoshoot location to set things up and be ready in the Bryte Tee!

  • 1. Find the assigned space to set up the portable studio

    • If you can't find the place in career center or at career fairs, you can always ask the career center members.
    • Make sure there's no strong and uneven lighting at the space.
    • Make sure there are power outlets nearby to power the lighting (you can use extension cords)

  • 2. Set up the equipment according to "Equipment Setup" section

    • Start from the Chromakey
    • Background light
    • Stool/Chair
    • Key Light
    • Reflector
    • Your DSLR & Tripod
    • Waiting Area
    • If you need help with anything (finding a chair, helping with setting up waiting area..), ask a career center members for help.

  • 3. Take some pictures for testing, and check for the following points

    • You will need a person to sit on the stool for you to test the photo. You could either set timer on your DSLR to take photos of your own, or to ask a career center member for help.
    • Check lighting on the face - Make sure the position of every piece of equipment is correct.
    • Check brightness - Adjust ISO to make sure the picture is as bright as the given example
    • Check Color - Make sure to adjust the white balance (WB) if Auto White Balance (AWB) does not look natural
    • Check Angle - Make sure the camera is not lower than the client's eye level

  • 4. Slack your trainer a picture of all the things after being set up

    • Make sure to slack your trainer a picture of photoshoot.

After the photoshoot

Clean up the space make sure it looks like what it was before the photoshoot.

Pack up all the equipment. Make sure to put light bulbs in the plastic packages if came with.

>> 8. Upload the Photo

The photos need to be uploaded to Dropbox on the same day. Make sure to contact your trainer to get the Dropbox credentials

Upload Instruction

  1. Go through the photos you took on a computer, delete all the blurry photos, and all the photos where client's eyes are closed, or not focused on the camera.
  2. Go to Dropbox, and log in with the credential.
  3. enter PHOTO folder, and locate the photoshoot folder inside PHOTO folder, there will be folders named with all the clients' email addresses from that photoshoot
  4. Upload all the JPEG photos under corresponding folders with the name being email addresses.
